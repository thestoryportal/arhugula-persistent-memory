## §2 — What this means
1. **Same-subject other-attribute drift is a property of MEMIT-class subject-token editing IN GENERAL, not a Qwen defect.** The original MEMIT "locality/specificity" is the **cross-entity** axis (different subjects) — which the harness confirms GPT-J passes (KL≈0). The paper never measured or controlled **same-subject, different-attribute** consistency. A literature-clean MEMIT edit can and does corrupt other attributes of the edited subject.
2. **The same-subject drift metric is NOT a calibrated absolute discriminator of clean-vs-broken.** There is no anchored "clean floor": the one known-clean reference edit (GPT-J) reads HIGH. So a low reading cannot be interpreted as "clean/attribute-local" in absolute terms, and a high reading is not evidence of a model defect.
3. **v1.8's ROUTE-MODEL premise is UNDERCUT.** v1.8 concluded the Qwen entanglement was "substantially a cfb-v3 corpus artifact" because cfb-v4 (structurally diverse) read low (0.20), and routed to a model-hunt "with the calibrated probe." But GPT-J on structurally-diverse CounterFact facts reads HIGH (2.48). So **"diverse corpus ⇒ low drift" does not generalize across models** — the inference behind v1.8 fails its own positive control.
4. **The Qwen-cfb-v4 low drift (0.20) is now the UNEXPLAINED OUTLIER** — low against BOTH GPT-J (high) and Qwen-cfb-v3 (high). Two live hypotheses, which the current instrument cannot separate:
   - **H-MODEL:** Qwen2.5-7B is genuinely more same-subject-local than GPT-J — a real, valuable property that would make Qwen the lead for multi-field LLM-as-DB.
   - **H-PROBE:** cfb-v4's particular subjects/targets/probes are drift-resistant — a probe artifact, in which case v1.8's "corpus artifact" verdict is itself not robust.

## §2.5 — Primary-source grounding (MEMIT paper, Meng et al., ICLR 2023; arXiv 2210.07229)
Confirmed against the paper directly — the same-subject locality gap is in the original method's evaluation, not an artifact of our reading:
- **The paper DOES mass-edit the GPT models** (GPT-J-6B, GPT-NeoX-20B, GPT2-XL), up to **10,000** facts on CounterFact + zsRE. So "the same editing on GPT" was done; the difference is purely *which locality axis was measured*.
- **Its specificity metric (Neighborhood Success, NS) is CROSS-ENTITY.** Verbatim (§5.2.2): *"For testing specificity, Neighborhood Success (NS) is defined similarly, but we check the probability G assigns to the correct answer oᶜᵢ (instead of oᵢ), given prompts about **distinct but semantically-related subjects** (instead of sᵢ)."* Conceptually (§3), for the very edit we used in Gate A: *"to test for specificity, we can ask about **similar subjects** for which memories should not change: 'What sport does Kobe Bryant play? What does Magic Johnson play?'"* — i.e. OTHER athletes, never other facts about Michael Jordan. zsRE specificity = *"argmax accuracy on a **randomly-sampled unrelated fact** that should not have changed."* "Bleedover" in the paper is exclusively this cross-entity regurgitation sense.
- **Same-subject, different-attribute preservation is NEVER measured by MEMIT.** The metric set is ES (efficacy), PS (paraphrase/generalization), NS (cross-entity specificity), S (harmonic mean), RS ("Consistency" = TF-IDF of text generated about the subject vs a Wikipedia reference about the **NEW** object — neutral-to-rewarding of bleed, not penalizing), GE (fluency). No metric, table, or discussion tests whether editing one attribute of an entity corrupts that entity's other attributes.
- **Our Gate A is therefore consistent with the paper, not in conflict with it.** The paper reports GPT-J at 10k edits with **NS = 83.5** (high/clean); Gate A independently reproduced GPT-J as clean on that same cross-entity axis (KL≈0.0001). The high same-subject drift (2.48) sits on an axis MEMIT is silent on. Hence GPT-J's "clean MEMIT model" reputation says nothing about same-subject locality — confirming there is no literature-anchored clean floor for the axis Probe-B measures.

### §2.5.1 — The lineage is NOT fully silent: ROME named this as "essence drift" (refines the claim above)
The MEMIT predecessor ROME (Meng et al., "Locating and Editing Factual Associations in GPT", arXiv 2202.05262) is the one place in this lineage that *acknowledges* same-subject collateral — but only qualitatively, and only partially controls it:
- It names the phenomenon **"essence drift"**: editing a fact can change the subject's other/essential properties. Verbatim: *"FT+L's generation exhibits a phenomenon we call essence drift … This is a nuanced form of bleedover that is **hard to detect quantitatively but easier to spot qualitatively**."* So the lineage knew the failure mode existed, deemed it hard to quantify, and never built a multi-attribute metric for it — Probe-B is essentially the quantification ROME called hard.
- It *partially regularizes* against it via a KL term in z-optimization (Eqn. 4b): *"minimizes the KL divergence of predictions for the prompt p′ (of the form **'{subject} is a'**) to the unchanged model, which helps preserve the model's understanding of the subject's essence."*
- **MEMIT inherits exactly this regularizer** (`kl_factor: 0.0625` in the GPT-J hparams; the same `"{} is a"` essence-KL term in `compute_z`). Gate A shows it is INSUFFICIENT: GPT-J still drifts at KL 2.48. The single coarse essence probe ("what kind of thing is this") protects the subject's category but NOT specific attributes (Eiffel's designer/material/year). This strengthens the finding — even the lineage's own essence safeguard does not prevent same-subject multi-attribute bleed.
- ROME also notes essence preservation is **model-dependent** ("GPT-J … preserve[s] essence much better than GPT-2 XL, perhaps due to its larger memory capacity") — i.e. the H-MODEL axis is anticipated by the lineage; S2.41 ARM X1 tests it directly.
- **Corrected claim:** same-subject locality is *named but not metricized* (ROME: "essence drift", qualitative + 1-probe regularizer) and *not measured at all* by MEMIT (cross-entity NS only). It remains an **un-metricized, under-controlled failure mode of MEMIT-class weight editing** — exactly the gap an LLM-as-database must close.

### §2.5.2 — A weight-preserving alternative exists: GRACE gets same-subject locality by construction
GRACE ("Aging with GRACE: Lifelong Model Editing with Discrete Key-Value Adaptors", Hartvigsen et al., NeurIPS 2023, arXiv 2211.11031; scaffolded on-NV at `grace_dry_run/`) does **not modify weights**: *"GRACE serves as a codebook in which edits are stored … ε-balls around cached edits. GRACE then only applies for inputs near existing keys … and leaves correct model behaviors intact … codebooks leave model weights unaltered."*
- An edit caches a key (activation at the edited prompt) → value, gated by a deferral radius ε. Inputs outside every ε-ball run the **frozen** base model. So editing "Eiffel located-in → Rome" leaves "Eiffel's designer" untouched **by construction** — the MEMIT-style same-subject bleed cannot occur, because no weights moved.
- Its locality metric is **TRR (Test Retention Rate)** = retention on the model's original test data (general held-out retention; a broad cousin of cross-entity specificity), plus ERR for prior edits. Still not a same-subject-attribute metric — GRACE simply forecloses the failure mode rather than measuring it.
- Trade-off (why it is a reframe, not a free win): GRACE is a **retrieval patch, not knowledge integration**. Generalization is whatever falls inside ε (too large → false-positive bleed; too small → paraphrases miss). For a multi-field LLM-as-DB this may be the *right* shape — independent per-field key-value writes with locality guaranteed — but it is a key-value store bolted onto a frozen model, not edited model knowledge.

### §2.5.3 — External-literature scan (operator-supplied, S2.40 close): the phenomenon is named, and our instrument is independently validated
Full scan in `s240_literature_scan.md` (load-bearing claims PDF-verified). Three points materially affect the v1.9/v1.8 framing:
- **The phenomenon has a literature name: "Ripple Effect in the Same Entity"** — *"editing knowledge about an entity potentially damages the model's memory of other facts related to that entity"* (ripple paper, arXiv 2403.07825; cites the RippleEdits line, Li et al. 2023b / Yao et al. 2023). Our Gate A same-subject drift is this effect; **RippleEdits** is the external benchmark of record. The MiuLab survey (arXiv 2406.01436, EMNLP 2024 Findings) gives the same failure example (editing the Louvre's location bleeds "London" into other Louvre facts). So same-subject collateral is a recognized, under-controlled problem — not a harness artifact.
- **Our Probe-B metric is independently endorsed.** "Are We Evaluating the Edit Locality of LLM Model Editing Properly?" (arXiv 2601.17343, Liu et al. 2026) argues existing specificity metrics are *"weakly correlated with the strength of specificity regularizers"* and *"can report high specificity even when the model's behavior with respect to unrelated queries has substantially changed"* — and proposes precisely our metric: *"behavioral deviation between the model before and after editing … distributional and ranking-based metrics derived from model query logits … ground-truth-free … continuous."* The survey's "Knowledge Distortion" (JS-divergence of pre/post object distributions) is a sibling. This **retroactively certifies the Gate A instrument** and strengthens, not weakens, the HALT: the field is independently concluding that standard locality eval is inadequate.
- **A within-MEMIT mechanism clue aligned to v1.7.2.** BLUE (arXiv 2502.03748) runs our exact layer sets and finds that under residual distribution the *first* critical layer contributes <0.1 to the edit while the *last* contributes ~1.0 — i.e. our front-loaded-L4 large-norm write (v1.7.2) is **low-contribution and error-inducing**, with weight-error growing in batch size and edit-sequence length (the multi-field/sequential DB regime). This converts v1.7.2 from a description into a testable fix (suppress the distributed early-layer residual; boundary-layer/computed-residual update) — carried to S2.41 as a candidate arm. (BLUE's reported specificity is "comparable"; same-subject-drift reduction is a hypothesis to test, not an established result.)
